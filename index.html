Abstract

We propose PreciseFlow, a training-free framework for stable and efficient inversion-free, flow-based video editing. Inversion-free editing methods have recently shown impressive efficiency and structure preservation in images by directly steering the sampling trajectory with an editing signal. However, extending this paradigm to videos remains challenging, often failing in multi-object scenes or with increased frame counts. We identify the root cause as the instability of the editing signal in high-dimensional video latent spaces, which arises from imprecise spatial localization and length-induced amplitude attenuation.

To overcome this challenge, PreciseFlow explicitly anchors both where to edit and how strongly to edit. It introduces Semantic Alignment Regulation, which enforces consistent alignment between textual guidance and spatial regions, and Dynamic Amplitude Regulation, which adaptively preserves sufficient editing strength. Together, these mechanisms stabilize the editing signal and guide the flow-based evolution toward the desired target distribution. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PreciseFlow achieves more faithful, temporally coherent, and computationally efficient video editing across challenging multi-object and fast-motion scenarios.

Problem Statement

While FlowEdit offers an efficient inversion-free framework, its naive application to video leads to noticeable performance degradation. We investigate this ineffectiveness by qualitatively and quantitatively analyzing the editing signal ΔV, revealing two factors that contribute to its instability.


(a) Imprecise Localization: Editing signal leaks to wrong regions. (b) Weakened Amplitude: Signal fades with more frames.

Method

PreciseFlow introduces two key mechanisms: Semantic Alignment Regulation (SAR) anchors where to edit, and Dynamic Amplitude Regulation (DAR) anchors how strongly to edit. Together, they stabilize the editing signal throughout the flow-based generation process.


(a) Overview of the editing process with SAR and DAR. (b) Cross-attention modulation at token and spatio-temporal levels. (c) Signal amplification using normalized importance map.


Semantic Alignment Regulation

SAR regulates cross-attention maps at both token and spatio-temporal levels to prevent semantic leakage in multi-object scenes. This ensures the editing signal remains consistently aligned with target semantics across all frames.


Dynamic Amplitude Regulation

DAR generates a normalized importance map from the editing signal itself, then uses it to adaptively amplify regions with significant semantic changes. This maintains sufficient editing strength even in longer video sequences.
